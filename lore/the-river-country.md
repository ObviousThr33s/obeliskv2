# The River Country

Far west of the mountains there is a place that some call home and nobody
calls anything else, because there is nobody else near enough to name it. The
salmon are near. The river is near — her great river, older than the saying of
it — and she comes down out of the high country the color of slate and goes on
past the people without asking after them, which is how they have come to love
her. She does not need watching to keep running. They have learned that from
her, and it is most of what they know.

They are few. This is not a wound. Empires stand everywhere a person might
walk, it would seem — everywhere but here, for now — and the few who live by the
river have noticed that being too small to conquer is very like being free. No
one comes for a handful of houses and a fish weir and a long memory. So they
keep small on purpose. They pare the year down to what stands. When a thing
arrives and asks to stay, they hold it up against the light and ask the keeping
question — does it earn its place, or are we only used to it? — and if it does
not spark, they thank it, and they carry it out.

What they keep is little, and it is this.

They keep scribes, because a thing said and not held is a thing half-lost, and
the river takes enough already without taking the words too. The scribe's work
is quiet. He does not invent. He waits at the edge of a moment and asks whether
there is anything true to set down, and if there is he sets it down, and if
there is not he lets the silence be — for a page with nothing to say is allowed
to say nothing, and that is not a failure of the page.

And they keep care for the sick. This above the rest. A people is not the
strong ones standing; a people is what it does for the one who cannot stand.
When the fever comes to a house the others come to it too, with water and with
sitting-up and with their hands, and they do kindly, and they do not ask first
whether it is owed. This is the whole of their law, if they have a law. Be few,
be kind, keep the words, tend the sick.

There is a place in the wood the elders will lead you to once, and only once,
walking slow. Bones sit in the water there, pale and patient, and the water is
very clear over them. You may lean down to it. You may look as long as you
like. But you do not, under any circumstance, touch what lies in that water —
not for keeping, not for kindness, not for any reason the heart will offer you,
and it will offer you several. Remember the boy. He was not wicked. That is the
hard part of the telling, the part the children always stumble on: he was not
wicked at all. He thought it was all his, and he thought so in good nature, and
the forest spirit did not care for the difference between greed and good nature
because the forest spirit had never been a person and did not grade on
intention. Good nature is no shield in that wood. Only the not-touching is.

Any good father knows this, and tells it the same way every father has told it:
jaw set, voice gone soft, the two together — because the clench is the fear and
the softness is the love, and a child should learn early that the two arrive in
the same breath and that this is not a contradiction. He does not raise his
voice. You do not raise your voice for the things that matter most. You lower
it, until the child has to lean in to hear, the way you lean down to the water,
and you say: do not go there. And then, because that is too heavy a thing to
leave a child holding alone, you take his hand and you walk him back out of the
wood, toward the river, toward the houses, toward the few warm lights of a
place too small to be wanted by anyone but the salmon — and her, of course.
Always her. Going on past, the color of slate, not asking after you, keeping
you anyway.
